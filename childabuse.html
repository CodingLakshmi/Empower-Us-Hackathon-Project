What is Child Abuse?

Child abuse is the physical maltreatment or sexual molestation of a child. This can result in harm, potential for harm, and threat of harm to a child by an adult. Child abuse can impact brain development and has effects on physical and mental health.

Types of Child Abuse:

  • Physical
    This is any deliberate physical force or action, by a parent or caregiver, which results, or could result, in injury to a child. This can include pushing, choking, slapping, beating, burning, biting, kicking, painful grabbing and punching.
  • Sexual
    This is when a child is used for the sexual pleasure of an adult or an older child. This includes sexual intercourse, sharing sexual photos and exposing a child private parts, fondling for sexual purposes, watching a child undress for satisfaction, forcing a child to watch pornographic videos.
  • Emotional
    This is the act of controlling a child by using emotions to embarrass, shame or criticize them. This may also include rejecting the child and isolating them.
  • Medical
    This is when a parent, guardian or care-giver, forces medical care on a child. This may include forcing the child to take excessive medicine.

Solutions to Child Abuse:

  • Physical
    As a parent, you should trust your child and listen to what they tell you. Sometimes children may find it difficult to open up to their parents or guardians when they’re being mistreated. It is important that you actively listen to them when they decide to let you know of what’s happening and seek help as soon as possible. Let your child know you care and you’re willing to help them Affirm your love for your child and show them you understand what they’re going through. Show empathy and have an open door policy for them. Let your child know that what’s happening to them is not their fault. Applaud your child for taking the bold step to report. As a child, you can tell trusted friends and family about your situation. You can also report your abuse.
  • Sexual
    You must understand that your body is yours and yours only. You have the right to keep your body private. You are allowed to refuse any kind of touch from another person. You can say no to anyone who wants to keep something “secret”. You are allowed to disobey and run if a stranger tries to touch you. Be aware of your surroundings and avoid isolated areas. If you have a phone, keep it with you.
  • Emotional
    Try to stay calm. Remember that it is not your fault, that you don’t deserve to be emotionally abused, and that if you are being abused, then it is wrong. Do not try to react or respond. This will have a negative effect on your mental health. The best way to deal with the abuser is to ignore them. You should maintain a physical distance, and distract yourself with something you enjoy. Find patterns in your abusive parent or caretaker. The key is to find out the warning signs, and try to logically talk to them. Tell them that whatever they are doing is making you feel bad. Don’t keep your feelings bottled up. Try to stay away from your abuser because listening to them will hurt your health. Find excuses to leave the house, like volunteering and being busy. This way, when you see the warning signs, you can leave the house easily.
  • Medical
    If you think you need medicine, find out the amount of medicine that you need to take. If you don’t need medicine, you shouldn’t be forced to take medicine. If you are being forced to take more medicine than necessary, then you are being abused. You should try to tell your caretaker that they are giving you an excess amount of medicine. If they continue to give you too much medicine, you should contact a trusted adult or report your abuser.

Signs of Abuse Adults Should Look For:

  • Withdrawal from friends
  • Withdrawal from usual activities
  • Changes in behavior:
    • Aggression
    • Anger
    • Hostility
    • Hyperactivity
    • Rebellious
    • Defiant
  • Changes in school performance
  • A lack of supervision
  • An excess amount of absences from school
  • Reluctance to leave school
  • Attempts at running away
  • Self harm or attempts at suicide

Signs of Abuse Kids Should Look For:

Do you:

  • Feel like you lack self-confidence or self-esteem?
  • Feel depressed and lonely?
  • Seek affection?
  • Have difficulty maintaining relationships?
  • Have challenges trusting somebody, especially an adult?
  • Find violence as a normal part of relationships?
  • Have a hard time dealing with stress and frustrations?

Does your parent or caregivier:

  • Show little concern for you
  • Blames you for their problems?
  • Consistently describes you as “worthless”, or “evil”?
  • Limit your contact with others?
  • Give you injuries, such as bruises, fractures, or burns?

Places to Contact

  • You can tell a trusted adult. For example, a school counselor, teacher, coach, or someone in your friend’s family.
  • If you are in true danger, call 911
  • Reach out, and for kids in the US and Canada, you can call 1-800-4-A-CHILD. Remember that there are always people there to help kids who are being hurt.
  • For people outside the US and Canada, you can visit this helpline: https://www.childhelplineinternational.org/child-helplines/child-helpline-network/
    A helpline is a telephone service which offers help to those who call. These helpline services can offer advice and information to help those in need.
